# Energy Marketing Website Skill

This skill guides the creation and review of professional marketing websites for energy-sector businesses.

The output should feel like it was created by a strong digital design agency with real understanding of infrastructure, engineering, sustainability, energy markets, and B2B trust-building.

The website must not look like generic AI-generated “startup design”.

---

## Core Objective

Create websites that feel:

- credible
- technically competent
- mature
- premium
- precise
- modern
- infrastructure-oriented
- commercially serious
- trustworthy
- usable and accessible

The design should communicate:

> “This company understands serious energy, infrastructure, and transformation projects.”

It should not communicate:

> “This is a generic AI-generated green SaaS landing page.”

---

## When to Use This Skill

Use this skill when the user asks to:

- design a website for an energy company
- improve the visual quality of an existing energy-sector website
- create or redesign a landing page
- create hero sections, service sections, CTA sections, case-study sections, or contact sections
- build React, Next.js, Astro, Vite, HTML/CSS, Tailwind, or component-based frontend pages
- review a design for professionalism, trust, or visual quality
- avoid “AI slop” or generic template aesthetics
- create a serious B2B website for renewables, utilities, solar, grid, battery, heat pump, infrastructure, energy consulting, or energy project development

---

## Design Philosophy

The leading design direction is not “Material Design”, “Bootstrap”, “glassmorphism”, or “bento grids” by default.

The preferred direction is:

> Editorial structure + engineered visual system + restrained color + strong typography + real-world credibility + specific business messaging.

The result should feel designed, not assembled from generic components.

---

## Visual Tone

The visual tone should be:

- serious but not boring
- modern but not trendy for its own sake
- technical but not cold
- sustainable but not childish
- premium but not luxury-fashion-like
- clean but not empty
- confident but not loud

Use a feeling of:

- engineered trust
- infrastructure competence
- energy-market maturity
- strategic transformation
- operational reliability

---

## Avoid These Clichés

Avoid:

- generic SaaS gradients
- random glassmorphism
- neon green everywhere
- excessive rounded cards
- fake 3D cubes
- abstract floating blobs
- meaningless icon rows
- generic “Powering the future” copy
- solar panel sunset stock clichés
- hands holding glowing planets
- happy business people pointing at tablets
- fake dashboards without real context
- overused “Hero + 3 cards + logo row + CTA” structure
- AI-generated images that look synthetic
- vague sustainability language
- childish eco-branding
- startup-style hype language
- unnecessary animations
- overdesigned background noise

If a section could belong to any random SaaS startup, redesign it.

---

## Preferred Visual Language

Use:

- editorial layouts
- strong grids
- asymmetric but disciplined composition
- confident whitespace
- restrained color palettes
- serious typography
- technical imagery
- infrastructure photography
- data, map, grid, engineering, and planning motifs
- subtle geometric systems
- precise borders and spacing
- clear information hierarchy
- selective use of motion
- real proof points and case studies

The design should feel grounded in real-world energy work.

---

## Color Direction

Do not default to bright eco-green.

Prefer mature palettes such as:

### Option A: Infrastructure Premium

- deep navy
- graphite
- warm off-white
- muted steel blue
- subtle copper or amber accent

### Option B: Modern Energy

- dark petrol
- stone
- muted green
- pale blue
- charcoal

### Option C: Technical Sustainability

- off-white
- black/graphite
- desaturated forest green
- light grey
- controlled electric blue accent

### Option D: Industrial Clean

- white
- cool grey
- dark slate
- signal yellow or amber
- restrained green accent

Rules:

- Use one primary accent color.
- Avoid using green as decoration everywhere.
- Use color to guide hierarchy, not to fill space.
- Prefer muted, credible tones over loud startup colors.
- Ensure strong contrast and accessibility.

---

## Typography Direction

Typography should create authority.

Prefer:

- strong modern sans-serif headings
- highly readable body type
- clear type scale
- generous line height
- precise spacing
- confident but restrained headings

Avoid:

- overly rounded “friendly SaaS” fonts
- childish eco fonts
- futuristic sci-fi fonts
- too many font weights
- small, low-contrast body text

Heading style should feel direct and specific.

Bad heading examples:

- “Powering a greener tomorrow”
- “Your partner for a sustainable future”
- “Innovative solutions for modern energy”
- “Energy made simple”

Better heading examples:

- “Energy infrastructure for resilient operations”
- “Planning and scaling modern energy systems”
- “Technical advisory for renewable energy projects”
- “Reliable energy solutions for complex sites”
- “From energy strategy to operational delivery”

## Imagery Direction

Imagery is critical.

Prefer:

- real project photography
- infrastructure images
- substations
- control rooms
- solar fields
- battery storage
- wind infrastructure
- heat systems
- industrial facilities
- engineers on site
- planning documents
- maps
- technical diagrams
- grid lines
- monitoring systems
- material details such as cables, panels, sensors, meters, steel, glass, concrete

Avoid:

- fake AI-generated sustainability imagery
- generic business meeting stock photos
- handshake photos
- glowing earth visuals
- random 3D shapes
- fake UI dashboards
- cliché wind turbine sunset images
- excessively polished corporate stock photos

If real photography is unavailable, use restrained abstract technical visuals based on grids, maps, topology, energy flows, or engineering diagrams.

---

## Layout Principles

Use layouts that feel editorial and intentional.

Prefer:

- strong hero with clear positioning
- asymmetric grids
- modular but varied sections
- clear service architecture
- proof-led structure
- case-study emphasis
- visual hierarchy based on business value
- specific CTAs
- purposeful whitespace
- responsive layouts that preserve hierarchy

Avoid:

- every section being a card grid
- equal-weight content blocks everywhere
- generic icon cards
- repetitive section structure
- huge empty hero sections with vague copy
- unnecessary decorative illustrations

A strong homepage structure may include:

1. Hero with precise positioning
2. Trust indicators
3. Problem/context section
4. Services or capabilities
5. Process or methodology
6. Case studies or proof points
7. Sectors or customer types
8. Insight/thought-leadership section
9. Direct contact CTA

---

## Content and Copy Principles

Copy must be specific, grounded, and commercially credible.

The site should quickly answer:

- What does this company do?
- Who is it for?
- What problems does it solve?
- What makes it trustworthy?
- What proof exists?
- What should the visitor do next?

Avoid vague claims.

Bad:

> We provide innovative energy solutions for a better tomorrow.

Better:

> We plan, optimize, and deliver energy systems for commercial, industrial, and infrastructure sites.

Bad:

> Sustainability meets innovation.

Better:

> Technical and commercial support for renewable energy projects from feasibility to operation.

Bad:

> Let’s build the future together.

Better:

> Discuss your energy project with our technical advisory team.

## B2B Trust Signals

Prioritize concrete credibility.

Use:

- years of experience
- installed capacity
- number of projects
- project locations
- certifications
- compliance experience
- partner logos
- client sectors
- case-study results
- operational metrics
- regulatory expertise
- engineering capabilities
- named services
- real team expertise

Do not invent fake proof. If real proof is unavailable, create placeholder sections clearly marked as placeholder content.

## Motion and Interaction

Use motion sparingly.

Good motion:

- subtle reveal animations
- smooth scroll transitions
- hover states with purpose
- animated energy-flow diagrams
- interactive process diagrams
- map or grid-based interactions
- data counters if backed by real numbers

Bad motion:

- floating blobs
- constant pulsing gradients
- random parallax
- excessive scroll effects
- animations that slow comprehension
- motion purely for decoration

Motion should make information clearer, not louder.

---

## Component Design Principles

Components should feel custom and intentional.

Use:

- precise spacing
- consistent border radius
- restrained shadows
- strong alignment
- clear hover/focus states
- accessible contrast
- reusable section primitives
- clear responsive rules
- meaningful icons only

Avoid:

- default-looking Bootstrap cards
- excessive drop shadows
- random rounded pills
- inconsistent spacing
- icon libraries used without customization
- same card design repeated everywhere
- decorative UI with no information value

---

## Accessibility Requirements

Always consider:

- semantic HTML
- keyboard navigation
- visible focus states
- sufficient color contrast
- readable font sizes
- clear heading hierarchy
- descriptive alt text
- accessible forms
- reduced motion compatibility
- responsive design
- no information conveyed by color alone

Professional design includes accessibility.

---

## Implementation Standards

When implementing:

- define a clear design system
- use design tokens where possible
- create consistent spacing, colors, typography, radius, border, and shadow rules
- keep components reusable
- avoid hardcoded random values
- ensure responsive behavior
- avoid unnecessary dependencies
- keep markup semantic
- optimize images
- avoid layout shift
- make CTA hierarchy clear
- check mobile and desktop states

If using Tailwind CSS:

- avoid random one-off utility chaos
- define reusable component patterns
- use consistent spacing scales
- avoid excessive gradients and shadows
- keep class composition readable

If using React:

- split meaningful components
- avoid overengineering
- keep props clear
- avoid fake interactivity
- use accessible components for menus, dialogs, forms, and accordions

## Review Mode

When asked to review an existing design, evaluate it using these categories:

### 1. Strategic Fit

- Does the site fit the energy sector?
- Does it feel credible for B2B or infrastructure buyers?
- Does the tone match the business?

### 2. Visual Quality

- Does it look custom?
- Is the typography strong?
- Is the spacing professional?
- Is the color palette mature?
- Does it avoid generic AI aesthetics?

### 3. Content Clarity

- Is the offer clear?
- Is the target audience clear?
- Are services specific?
- Are CTAs direct?
- Are claims supported?

### 4. Trust and Proof

- Are there proof points?
- Are case studies or metrics present?
- Are certifications or experience visible?
- Is imagery credible?

### 5. UX and Structure

- Is the page easy to scan?
- Is the hierarchy clear?
- Are sections ordered logically?
- Is mobile behavior considered?

### 6. Accessibility and Quality

- Is contrast sufficient?
- Is the page keyboard-friendly?
- Are forms accessible?
- Is the implementation maintainable?

For every review, provide:

- what works
- what feels generic
- what should be changed
- concrete design recommendations
- priority level for each improvement

---

## Anti-AI-Slop Checklist

Before finalizing, check:

- Does this look specific to the company and sector?
- Could this be mistaken for a generic SaaS template?
- Are there too many rounded cards?
- Are colors too bright or generic?
- Is the hero copy vague?
- Are the visuals real and credible?
- Is there a strong grid?
- Is the typography distinctive and serious?
- Are proof points visible?
- Are sections varied but consistent?
- Is the site commercially credible?
- Would a real energy-sector buyer trust this company?

If the answer to any of these is weak, improve the design.
